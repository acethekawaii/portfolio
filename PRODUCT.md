## Users

Hiring managers, engineering leads, and recruiters at companies **actively hiring remote mid-level software engineers**. They land here from a job application, a referral, or a GitHub/LinkedIn link, often skimming many candidates in one sitting. Their job-to-be-done: decide in under a minute whether this person is a credible full-stack engineer worth interviewing, then find the proof (projects, stack, contact) fast. Context is usually desktop during work hours, sometimes mobile. Secondary user: the candidate himself, sending the link as the single source of truth about his work.

## Product Purpose

A personal portfolio for a **mid-level full-stack software engineer** applying to remote roles. The candidate is backend-strong and architecture-minded; the target job list leans on the JS/SQL/NoSQL stack he already works in (Node/Next, PostgreSQL, MySQL, MongoDB, plus PHP). The site exists to convert a cold link into an interview by showing real shipped, production-grade work (systems that real users depend on), a legible skill set, and a hireable, low-risk professional presence. Success = a recruiter reaches the contact/resume CTA convinced, and the site itself (built in Next.js) is read as a working sample of the candidate's craft. The portfolio is both the message and the proof.

## Brand Personality

Competent first, funny second, never the other way around. Three words: **sharp, warm, self-aware**. The engineering is dead serious; the voice isn't. Picture a senior-leaning dev who ships production systems and still slips a pun into the PR description: dry wit, the occasional dad joke, light self-deprecating sarcasm. Humor is seasoning, not the meal. It lives in the microcopy (hover states, empty states, the 404, alt text, button labels, the footer, easter eggs), while the proof (projects, metrics, stack, the resume CTA) stays straight-faced. That split is the whole strategy: the joke only lands if the reviewer already believes this person can do the job, so the humor reads as confidence, not as compensating for thin work.

The motto "Failing to plan is planning to fail" stays, but it's allowed to wink at itself now (it earned that). Personality still shows through craft, motion, and detail, not loud theming. Target feeling: "this person is clearly good AND I'd actually enjoy working with them." The kawaii handle finally gets to mean something, subtly, never a costume.

**Voice guardrails.** Keep jokes short and skimmable (a recruiter scanning in 30s should get the point even if they miss the gag). Punch down at the code/the bugs/yourself, never at the reader or a tech stack they might love. One joke per surface, not a comedy set; relentless quipping reads as trying too hard. No memes, no exclamation-mark spam, no "lol". If a line has to choose between clear and clever, clear wins.

## Anti-references

- **Generic dev-template portfolios**: dark-mode + gradient-blob + "Hi, I'm X" hero, the interchangeable boilerplate everyone ships. Hard avoid.
- **Over-animated / gimmicky**: cursor trails, excessive parallax, motion for its own sake.
- **Corporate / sterile SaaS**: soulless, no human behind it.
- **Cluttered / maximalist**: too much at once, hard to scan.
- **Dark mode**: the candidate dislikes it. This is a **light-theme** site.
- **Humor that undercuts credibility**: jokes in the project metrics, stack list, or resume CTA; clown-college energy; trying-hard quip density. The work is the straight man. Wit lives in microcopy, not in the proof.
- Note the tension: "kawaii" handle licenses *wit and warmth*, not a cute/playful visual identity. The humor is verbal (copy), the visuals stay clean and grown-up. No mascots, sticker soup, or pastel-overload to "match" the jokes.

## Design Principles

1. **Hireable in 30 seconds.** Above-the-fold answers who/what/proof before any scrolling. Optimize the skim, not the deep read.
2. **The site is the sample.** Every interaction is read as engineering work; craft, performance, and accessibility are the argument, not decoration.
3. **Show shipped work, not adjectives.** Lead with concrete projects, stack, and outcomes over self-describing buzzwords.
4. **Warmth through wit, restraint through visuals.** Personality lives in the copy (a dry aside, a pun, a self-aware footer) and in motion, spacing, and detail, never in loud color or theming. The page looks calm and confident; the words have a sense of humor. One earns trust, the other earns a smile.
5. **Frictionless to act on.** Resume, GitHub, live demos, and contact are always one obvious click away.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG 2.1 AA baseline: body text ≥4.5:1 contrast, large text ≥3:1, full keyboard navigation, visible focus states, semantic landmarks. `prefers-reduced-motion` honored on every animation (the brief warns against over-animation, so motion stays purposeful and reduced-motion-safe). Light theme only. No special-needs requirements beyond standard AA.
